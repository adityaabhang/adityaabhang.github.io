What Did I Do?

In this lab, I gave a robot the ability to "see" and make decisions based on what it sees. Using a camera mounted on the robot, I programmed it to identify parts, measure them, and decide whether they are good or defective based on their size.

Goal: Use machine vision to classify parts as good or defective, and have the robot pick and sort them accordingly.

The Challenge

The robot needed to:

  • Capture an image of the part using its camera
  • Analyze the image to measure the part's size
  • Compare the size to a standard threshold
  • Decide if the part is good or defective
  • Pick and place the part in the correct bin

How Machine Vision Works

The robot uses a 4-step process to identify and classify parts:

Capture Image
Robot camera takes a photo of the part
2

3

The Vision Technology

I used mathematical techniques called "moments" to analyze part shapes and sizes:

Central Moments

A way to measure the size of a part. The "central moment" tells us the total area of the part in the image. For example, a larger nut has a larger central moment than a smaller nut.

Hu Moments

A set of seven special measurements that describe the shape of an object. These are powerful because they work even if the object is rotated or scaled. This makes shape recognition more reliable in real-world scenarios.

Why use these techniques? They allow the robot to recognize parts accurately, even with slight variations in lighting, position, or rotation.

Classification Strategy

The robot follows a decision tree to classify parts:

Step 1: Capture Grayscale Image

The camera captures a color image and converts it to grayscale (black and white).

Step 2: Convert to Binary

The image is converted to pure black and white using a threshold. Black represents the part, white represents the background.

Step 3: Calculate Total Area (M00)

The robot counts all the black pixels to get the total area of the part. This is called the "M00" measurement.

Step 4: Compare to Threshold

If M00 is above the threshold (1000), the robot checks if it meets the quality standard. If it does, it picks the part. If it's below the threshold or doesn't meet quality, it's marked as defective.

What I Learned

  • Computer Vision Basics: How computers "see" and interpret images
  • Image Processing: Techniques to convert and analyze images (grayscale, binary, thresholding)
  • Shape Recognition: Using mathematical moments to identify and classify objects
  • Quality Control: How vision systems automate quality checks in manufacturing
  • Decision Making: How to program robots to make decisions based on visual data

Why This Matters

Machine vision is transforming industries worldwide:

  • Manufacturing Quality: Cameras automatically inspect thousands of parts per day, catching defects humans might miss
  • Cost Savings: Fewer defective products reach customers, reducing waste and returns
  • Speed & Efficiency: Vision-based inspection happens in milliseconds, keeping production fast
  • Safety: Robots can inspect hazardous materials without putting humans at risk
  • Consistency: Machines always apply the same standards, unlike human inspectors who might vary day to day

Challenges I Overcame

During this lab, I faced and resolved several challenges:

  • Understanding Pixel Values: I initially confused which pixel values represented black vs. white, but quickly corrected this and understood the convention
  • Image Quality: The simulation had grainy textures that could affect measurements. I learned to account for these real-world imperfections
  • Threshold Tuning: Finding the right threshold value to accurately classify parts required testing and refinement
